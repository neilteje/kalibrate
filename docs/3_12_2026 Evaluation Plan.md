## **Section I: Evaluation Objectives**

Pop culture prediction markets, such as those on Kalshi, have grown significantly in popularity and trading volume. These fast-moving markets are driven by hype and sentiment, presenting challenges in forecasting The primary objective of our evaluation is to determine whether our forecasting agent outperforms simpler baseline approaches on our benchmark in terms of predictive quality, calibration, robustness, and efficiency. Our project is motivated by the idea that an agentic workflow by combining evidence gathering, reasoning, and synthesis should lead to stronger forecasts than direct one-shot prediction alone. 

Our evaluation addresses five research questions:

1. Does our agent outperform baseline methods overall?  
2. Does its advantage grow as task complexity increases, especially on questions involving ambiguous evidence, temporal judgment, or multi-step reasoning?  
3. What categories of tasks does it handle well, and where does it fail?  
4. Is it well calibrated, meaning its confidence estimates align with actual outcomes?  
5. What tradeoff does it make between forecast quality and computational cost, including runtime, tool use, and tokens?

A successful outcome would show that the full agent achieves stronger benchmark performance than relevant baselines, especially on harder tasks, while also maintaining better calibration and acceptable efficiency. Such results would validate our hypothesis that structured agentic reasoning improves forecasting quality beyond simpler prompting-based approaches. By contrast, results would be considered negative or inconclusive if the full agent fails to outperform baselines, shows only marginal or inconsistent gains, is poorly calibrated, or requires substantially more cost without meaningful quality improvement.

## **Section II: Benchmark Evaluation Metrics**

We evaluate our agent using a combination of predictive quality, correctness, efficiency, and reliability metrics. Since our benchmark is centered on probabilistic forecasting, our primary quality metrics are Brier Score and Log Loss, which measure both accuracy and calibration of forecast probabilities. For each task, if the agent outputs probability pp for a binary event with outcome y{0,1}, we compute Brier Score as (p-y)2 and log Loss as \-\[y\*log(p)+(1-y)log(1-p)\]. Lower  values are better for both metrics. We also compute the Task Success Rate, defined as the percentage of tasks for which the agent’s final binary forecast matches the resolved outcome. Together, these metrics capture not only whether the agent was right, but whether it was appropriately confident. In addition to forecast quality, we measure efficiency through runtime per task, number of API/tool calls, total token usage, and estimated cost per task. These metrics help determine whether the gains of the agentic system justify its added complexity. We also perform structured error analysis by labeling each failure according to categories such as incorrect forecast direction, overconfidence, evidence misuse, reasoning failure, tool-use failure, abstention failure, and formatting failure. This allows us to understand not just how often the agent fails, but how it fails. As secondary metrics, we evaluate robustness, consistency, generalization, and human usefulness. Robustness is measured by performance degradation on edge-case or perturbed tasks. Consistency is measured by variance in forecast probability and decision across repeated runs of the same task. Generalization is measured by performance on held-out or modified tasks that were not used in development. Human evaluation, if included, will assess explanation clarity, evidence relevance, and perceived trustworthiness on a small sample of outputs. Across all metrics, we will report overall aggregate performance as well as breakdowns by task category and difficulty. Good performance is defined as lower Brier Score and Log Loss, higher Task Success Rate, low severe-error frequency, and reasonable cost relative to baselines; poor performance is defined as weak calibration, baseline-level correctness, unstable repeated-run behavior, or large efficiency overhead without meaningful forecast improvement.

**Section III: Baseline Comparisons**  
We compare our forecasting agent against four baselines chosen to capture increasing levels of difficulty and realism. The first is a random/prior baseline, which outputs either a random binary prediction or a fixed probability such as 0.5 for all tasks. This baseline establishes the minimum performance floor and helps contextualize metrics such as Task Success Rate and Brier Score. The second is a single-prompt LLM baseline, where a strong language model receives the task and produces a final forecast in one shot, without iterative search, decomposition, or self-correction. This baseline is important because it tests whether our gains come from the agentic workflow rather than simply from using a capable model. We will implement this using the OpenAI Responses API, while restricting the model to a single direct response.  
The third is a tool-enabled frontier model baseline, which uses a current general-purpose model with search/tool access but without our custom forecasting pipeline. This provides a comparison against a strong off-the-shelf agentic system. We can implement this with a current OpenAI model through the Responses API, which supports built-in tools such as web search and file search, or with Anthropic’s Claude API, whose current documentation describes Claude 4.x-family models as the latest generation. The fourth baseline is an ablation of our own system, where we remove one or more key components such as retrieval, decomposition, evidence ranking, or reflection. This baseline helps us determine whether the improvements of the full system are actually caused by the architectural choices we introduce. All baselines will be evaluated on the same benchmark tasks, using the same output schema and metric calculations, so that comparisons remain fair and reproducible.

**Section IV: Experimental Setup**  
We plan to evaluate our agent on the same benchmark tasks using a standardized input and output schema. Each system will receive the same task description and must return a final forecast in a fixed format that includes at least a binary outcome and forecast probability. Every agent will be run on every task so that comparisons are directly aligned across the full benchmark. For non-deterministic systems, we will run 3 trials per task-agent combination and report both mean performance and variance across runs. Deterministic baselines will be run once. To reduce ordering effects, we will randomize both task order and agent evaluation order using a fixed experiment seed.  
Because LLM-based systems can vary across runs, we explicitly account for non-determinism by repeating trials under fixed settings and measuring variability in final prediction, confidence, runtime, and tool usage. Our primary reported results will use the average metric values across trials, while standard deviation will be reported to capture stability. We will also enforce common execution budgets such as maximum runtime, token usage, and tool calls so that no method has an unfair resource advantage.  
For each trial, we will log task metadata, agent configuration, model version, prompt version, timestamp, final prediction, forecast probability, correctness, Brier Score, Log Loss, runtime, API/tool calls, token usage, estimated cost, and completion status. For agentic systems, we will additionally log execution traces such as intermediate reasoning, retrieval outputs, tool queries, and revisions. Results will be stored in structured raw-run and summary tables, with detailed traces saved separately for later qualitative analysis. The evaluation pipeline will be implemented in Python, with pandas and NumPy for aggregation and metric computation, and a script-based harness to ensure that the entire evaluation can be reproduced from frozen benchmark data, fixed prompts, and versioned experiment configurations.

**Section V: Benchmark Validation**  
We will validate the quality of our benchmark by ensuring that every task is clearly written, well-scoped and paired with an objective resolution rule. Each item will include a precise question statement, explicit time horizon, and unambiguous success condition. Before inclusion in the final benchmark, tasks will be reviewed using a checklist to confirm that the event is identifiable, the wording is not ambiguous, and the outcome can be resolved consistently. We will also conduct pilot testing on a subset of tasks using simple baselines, stronger LLM baselines, and limited human review to identify unclear or poorly calibrated items. Tasks that are too easy, too ambiguous, or too difficult to separate methods meaningfully will be revised or removed.  
We aim to validate the benchmark coverage by ensuring that the task set spans multiple aspects of the forecasting domain, including different categories, evidence conditions, and reasoning requirements. We will also include meaningful difficulty variation by labeling tasks as easy, medium or hard and looking at those labels after pilot testing if needed. We also hope that our benchmark will contain enough tasks to support both overall performance comparisons as well as breakdowns by task category and difficulty.   
We will validate benchmark reliability by freezing the benchmark before the final evaluation and using the same tasks, labels, prompts, and metric calculations for every system. Success criteria will be objective wherever possible, and all scoring scripts will be shared across methods. We will test reproducibility by rerunning a subset of experiments under the same settings and checking whether aggregate results remain stable. During benchmark construction, multiple reviewers will also examine task wording and resolution rules to detect disagreement; tasks with inconsistent interpretation will be rewritten or excluded. Together, these steps help ensure that benchmark results are consistent, reproducible, and based on clear evaluation criteria rather than accidental benchmark artifacts.  
**Section VI: Expected Results**  
Since we do not yet have quantitative results, we instead describe the specific patterns we expect to observe. We expect the full agent to outperform the random/prior baseline and the single-prompt baseline on our primary forecast-quality metrics, especially Brier Score and Log Loss. We expect the largest improvements to appear on tasks that require evidence gathering, multi-step reasoning, and uncertainty handling, since these are the settings where an agentic workflow should provide the greatest advantage over direct prompting.  
We also expect the performance gap between the full agent and simpler baselines to grow as task difficulty increases. On easy, evidence-rich tasks, differences between systems may be modest. On harder tasks involving sparse signals, ambiguous evidence, or temporal reasoning, we expect the full agent to perform more strongly and to produce better-calibrated forecast probabilities. At the same time, we expect the full agent to be more expensive and slower than simpler baselines, though we expect this additional cost to be justified by improved predictive quality.

**Section VII: Limitations and Threats**  
Our evaluation has several important limitations. First, the benchmark measures forecasting quality in a controlled offline setting, so it will not fully capture real-world deployment factors such as changing user goals, live interaction with decision-makers, long-term adaptation, or performance under continuously evolving information streams. It also will not fully measure whether the agent is useful in a production workflow where timing, explanation quality, and trust may matter as much as raw prediction quality. In addition, our task design may introduce benchmark bias: if too many tasks come from the same types of events, rely on similar public signals, or reflect our own assumptions about what makes a question “hard,” then the benchmark may favor systems tuned to those patterns rather than truly general forecasting ability. A second limitation is that strong aggregate metrics may hide meaningful weaknesses, such as instability on rare categories, poor performance on edge cases, or high sensitivity to retrieval quality and prompt wording.  
Several threats could also confound validity. Performance differences may be driven partly by model version changes, API variability, non-deterministic outputs, unequal access to external information, or differences in tool budgets rather than the agent design itself. Results may also fail to generalize beyond our benchmark if future tasks are drawn from different domains, have longer time horizons, require richer domain expertise, or involve lower-quality evidence than the tasks we include here. Our evaluation therefore assumes that benchmark tasks are representative of the broader forecasting setting, that ground-truth resolutions are objective, and that repeated-run averages are a reasonable proxy for expected system performance. To mitigate these issues, we will freeze benchmark versions, standardize prompts and budgets, run multiple trials, report variance and error categories, validate task wording through pilot review, and break down results by category and difficulty instead of relying only on one overall score. In our conclusions, we will explicitly state that our findings support claims only within the scope of this benchmark and do not by themselves prove broad real-world superiority across all forecasting environment
